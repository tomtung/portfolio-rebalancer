
# Algorithmic Frameworks for Multi-Account Portfolio Rebalancing: A Comprehensive Analysis of Look-Through Optimization Under Constraints

## 1. Executive Summary and Problem Context

The evolution of modern portfolio management has increasingly shifted from a singular, account-centric view to a holistic, household-centric methodology. This paradigm shift, while theoretically superior for risk management and tax efficiency, introduces a class of combinatorial optimization problems that are significantly more complex than traditional single-account rebalancing. The "Household Portfolio Problem" requires the orchestration of multiple distinct pools of capital—taxable brokerage accounts, tax-deferred vehicles like IRAs and 401(k)s, and tax-exempt accounts like Roth IRAs—to achieve a unified asset allocation target.

The specific challenge addressed in this report is the development of a rebalancing algorithm capable of operating within a strictly constrained environment: a multi-account structure where cross-account transfers are prohibited, specific assets may be locked ("don't sell" or "don't buy"), and the asset allocation targets are defined not by direct holdings but by "look-through" exposures (e.g., the aggregate exposure to "US Equity" derived from a mix of ETFs, mutual funds, and individual stocks). Furthermore, the solution must be computationally feasible for a client-side JavaScript environment, necessitating a departure from heavy server-side mixed-integer quadratic programming (MIQP) solvers toward more lightweight, yet effective, Linear Programming (LP) or heuristic approaches.

This report establishes that while global optimality (in the sense of perfect mean-variance optimization) may be mathematically elusive given the non-convex nature of certain constraints and the computational limits of browser-based execution, effective drift reduction can be robustly achieved. By reformulating the rebalancing problem as a minimization of the L1-norm of allocation drift, we can leverage efficient Linear Programming solvers available in JavaScript. Complementing this, heuristic methods such as greedy marginal contribution algorithms and iterative water-filling strategies provide necessary "non-trivial" adjustments to handle edge cases, integer constraints, and the rigidities of the "no-transfer" rule. The synthesis of these approaches allows for the construction of a rebalancing engine that respects the rigid silos of individual accounts while optimizing the fluid risk profile of the aggregate household.

## 2. The Geometry of the Household Portfolio

To engineer a solution, one must first rigorously define the geometric and topological structure of the multi-account portfolio. Traditional Modern Portfolio Theory (MPT) assumes a frictionless plane where capital can flow freely between assets. The multi-account constraint transforms this plane into a series of disjoint feasible regions (silos), linked only by the shared objective function of the aggregate allocation.

### 2.1 The Silo Constraint Topology

The defining characteristic of this problem is the **Account Silo Constraint**. In a unified portfolio, if the aggregate allocation requires selling $\$10,000$ of Fixed Income and purchasing $\$10,000$ of Equities, the transaction is a simple swap. However, in a multi-account context, the Fixed Income may reside in Account $A$ (e.g., a 401(k) with limited investment options) while the Equities must be purchased in Account $B$ (e.g., a taxable brokerage account).

Because cross-account transfers are prohibited—a restriction rooted in tax regulations (contribution limits, early withdrawal penalties) and practical friction—the rebalancing operation must be **locally self-financing**. That is, the sum of capital generated by sales in Account $n$ must exactly match the sum of capital consumed by purchases in Account $n$ (minus transaction costs).

$$\sum_{i \in \mathcal{A}_n} \text{Sell}_i = \sum_{j \in \mathcal{A}_n} \text{Buy}_j \quad \forall n \in \{1, \dots, N\}$$

This constraint decouples the global objective (reduce total portfolio drift) from the local means of execution. It creates scenarios where the global optimal move (buy Asset $X$) is locally impossible (no cash in the account that can hold Asset $X$). Consequently, the algorithm serves not merely as an optimizer but as an arbitration engine, seeking the **Best Available Approximation** of the target allocation subject to the immobility of capital.1

### 2.2 Look-Through Asset Allocation

The complexity is further compounded by the requirement for "look-through" allocation. In institutional finance, this is often referred to as "factor exposure" or "exposure management." The user does not target a 10% weight in "Vanguard Total World Stock ETF (VT)"; rather, they target specific weights in "North American Equity," "European Equity," "Emerging Markets," and "Global Bonds." Since `VT` contains all of these, a single transaction in one ticker affects multiple target buckets simultaneously.

Mathematically, this introduces an **Exposure Matrix** $E$, of dimensions $M \times K$, where $M$ is the number of tradeable assets (tickers) and $K$ is the number of asset classes (categories). The element $E_{i,k}$ represents the percentage of Asset $i$ that consists of Class $k$.

The aggregate exposure of the portfolio to Class $k$ is calculated as:

$$\text{Exposure}_k = \sum_{n=1}^{N} \sum_{i=1}^{M} H_{n,i} \cdot E_{i,k}$$

where $H_{n,i}$ is the dollar value of Asset $i$ held in Account $n$.

This transformation means the algorithm cannot simply "top up" buckets. Buying an aggregate bond fund to satisfy a "US Treasury" deficit might inadvertently create a "Corporate Bond" surplus. The algorithm must solve an inverse problem: finding the combination of tradeable assets $\Delta H$ that minimizes the error vector between the current aggregate exposure vector and the target vector, subject to the constraint that assets typically bundle exposures in fixed ratios.3

### 2.3 The Drift Minimization Objective

While classical MPT seeks to maximize the Sharpe ratio (Mean-Variance Optimization), the practical mandate of a rebalancing engine is to minimize **Tracking Error** or **Allocation Drift** relative to a pre-defined policy benchmark. The "gap" mentioned in the user query is the distance between the current portfolio state and the target state.

In a constrained environment, we cannot assume the target is perfectly achievable. For instance, if the target requires 20% exposure to "Crypto Assets" but no account in the portfolio allows purchasing them (or they are marked "don't buy"), the drift cannot be zeroed out. Therefore, the objective function must be constructed to find the solution that minimizes the **norm of the drift vector**, essentially asking: "What is the closest we can get to the target given the constraints?"

The choice of norm is critical. Minimizing the squared error (L2 norm) corresponds to minimizing variance and is standard in quadratic programming. However, minimizing the absolute error (L1 norm) is often preferred for rebalancing because it is less sensitive to outliers, has a direct interpretation (sum of dollars misplaced), and, crucially, can be formulated as a Linear Program (LP), which is solvable with greater speed and stability in a JavaScript environment compared to non-linear solvers.4

## 3. Mathematical Formulation

To build a robust algorithm, we must translate the qualitative requirements into a rigorous mathematical model. We define the problem as a Constrained Optimization problem.

### 3.1 Notation

-   $N$: Number of accounts.
    
-   $M$: Number of unique tradeable assets (tickers).
    
-   $K$: Number of look-through asset classes.
    
-   $h_{n,i}$: Current holdings (in dollars) of asset $i$ in account $n$.
    
-   $x_{n,i}^+$: Dollars of asset $i$ bought in account $n$ (Decision Variable).
    
-   $x_{n,i}^-$: Dollars of asset $i$ sold in account $n$ (Decision Variable).
    
-   $E_{i,k}$: Fractional exposure of asset $i$ to asset class $k$ ($0 \le E_{i,k} \le 1$).
    
-   $T_k$: Target allocation percentage for asset class $k$.
    
-   $W$: Total wealth of the portfolio ($W = \sum \sum h_{n,i}$).
    
-   $L_{n,i}^{sell}$: Binary constraint; 1 if asset $i$ in account $n$ cannot be sold ("Don't Sell").
    
-   $L_{n,i}^{buy}$: Binary constraint; 1 if asset $i$ in account $n$ cannot be bought ("Don't Buy").
    

### 3.2 The Core Linear Program (LP) Model

We aim to minimize the deviation between the final exposure and the target exposure. Since standard LP solvers require linear objective functions, we linearize the absolute deviation using slack variables.

Objective Function:

Minimize total absolute drift:

$$\min Z = \sum_{k=1}^{K} (d_k^+ + d_k^-)$$

Here, $d_k^+$ represents the amount by which Class $k$ is overweight (above target), and $d_k^-$ represents the amount it is underweight (below target). By minimizing the sum of these non-negative variables, we effectively compress the portfolio's deviation toward zero.5

**Constraints:**

1.  Look-Through Balance Equations (Soft Constraints):
    
    For each asset class $k$, the post-trade exposure must equal the target exposure plus/minus the deviation:
    
    $$\sum_{n=1}^{N} \sum_{i=1}^{M} (h_{n,i} + x_{n,i}^+ - x_{n,i}^-) \cdot E_{i,k} = T_k \cdot W + d_k^+ - d_k^-$$
    
    This is the engine of the "look-through" logic. It aggregates the impact of every buy/sell across all accounts and maps it to the factor level. The solver is forced to adjust $x$ values to minimize $d$ values.
    
2.  Self-Financing / No-Transfer Constraints (Hard Constraints):
    
    For each account $n$, the total cash used for buys must equal the total cash generated from sells (plus any initial cash $C_n$):
    
    $$\sum_{i=1}^{M} x_{n,i}^+ = \sum_{i=1}^{M} x_{n,i}^- + C_n$$
    
    This enforces the "silo" wall. Capital cannot cross the index $n$.
    
3.  Inventory Constraints:
    
    You cannot sell more than you own:
    
    $$x_{n,i}^- \le h_{n,i} \quad \forall n, i$$
    
4.  "Don't Sell" Constraints:
    
    If the user has locked asset $i$ in account $n$ from selling ($L_{n,i}^{sell} = 1$):
    
    $$x_{n,i}^- = 0$$
    
5.  "Don't Buy" Constraints:
    
    If the user has locked asset $i$ in account $n$ from buying ($L_{n,i}^{buy} = 1$):
    
    $$x_{n,i}^+ = 0$$
    
6.  Non-Negativity:
    
    $$x_{n,i}^+, x_{n,i}^-, d_k^+, d_k^- \ge 0$$
    

### 3.3 Handling Infeasibility with Elastic Constraints

In a highly constrained multi-account scenario, a perfect solution ($Z=0$) is often impossible. For example, if the portfolio is 100% Bonds, and all Bonds are marked "Don't Sell," and the target is 60% Stocks, the solver cannot reduce the Bond weight. Ideally, it should buy Stocks with any available cash, but it cannot sell Bonds.

Standard LP solvers might return "Infeasible" if the constraints contradict. However, the formulation above using $d_k^+$ and $d_k^-$ acts as an **Elastic Constraint** (or "Goal Programming" formulation). The solver will essentially say: "I cannot reduce the Bond overweight because of the hard constraint $x^- = 0$, so I will accept a large value for $d_{Bond}^+$ and minimize $d$ elsewhere." This ensures the algorithm always returns the _best possible_ feasible rebalance, rather than crashing.7

### 3.4 Integration of Transaction Costs

While not explicitly requested as a primary minimization target, preventing excessive churn is vital ("non-trivial adjustments"). We can augment the objective function with a penalty term for turnover:

$$\min Z = \sum_{k=1}^{K} (d_k^+ + d_k^-) + \lambda \sum_{n,i} (x_{n,i}^+ + x_{n,i}^-)$$

Where $\lambda$ is a small scalar (e.g., $0.001$). This "regularization" ensures that if two solutions have equal drift reduction, the solver chooses the one with less trading volume. This prevents "wash" trades where the solver might unnecessarily sell Asset A to buy Asset B just to reach the same exposure balance.4

## 4. Algorithmic Approach I: Linear Programming Implementation

The theoretical model defined above translates effectively into a JavaScript environment. The library `javascript-lp-solver` is identified as the optimal tool for this task due to its pure JS implementation (no WebAssembly overhead), support for JSON model definitions, and adequate performance for typical portfolio sizes ($< 1000$ variables).10

### 4.1 Constructing the JSON Model

The implementation requires dynamically generating a large JSON object representing the problem.

#### Step 1: Variable Generation

For every asset in every account, we generate two variables.

-   `buy_acc1_SPY`: Represents $x_{1, SPY}^+$.
    
-   `sell_acc1_SPY`: Represents $x_{1, SPY}^-$.
    

#### Step 2: Objective Coefficient Definition

The objective is defined implicitly by the cost of the slack variables. However, `javascript-lp-solver` typically minimizes a specific attribute. A clever reformulation is required for the library's specific syntax. We define the "cost" of deviation variables as 1.

#### Step 3: Constraint Definition

The `constraints` object handles the logic.

-   **Balance Constraint:** `{"acc1_cash": { "equal": 0 }}`.
    
    -   `buy_acc1_SPY` contributes `-1` to `acc1_cash`.
        
    -   `sell_acc1_SPY` contributes `+1` to `acc1_cash`.
        
-   **Target Constraint:** `{"target_US_Equity": { "equal": TargetAmount }}`.
    
    -   This is tricky because LP constraints are usually hard limits ($ \le $ or $ \ge $). To implement the L1 minimization ($|Actual - Target|$), we use the standard trick of splitting the error into positive and negative parts.
        
    -   We add variables `drift_Equity_Pos` and `drift_Equity_Neg`.
        
    -   Constraint: `ActualExposure - drift_Equity_Pos + drift_Equity_Neg = TargetAmount`.
        
    -   Objective: Minimize `sum(drift_..._Pos + drift_..._Neg)`.
        

### 4.2 Handling Look-Through in the Solver

The power of this approach lies in the variable definitions. When defining `buy_acc1_VT` (Vanguard World Stock), we impute its exposures directly into the coefficients:

JavaScript

```
// Pseudo-code structure for javascript-lp-solver model
variables: {
  "buy_acc1_VT": {
    "exposure_US_Equity": 0.60,  // VT is 60% US
    "exposure_Intl_Equity": 0.40, // VT is 40% Intl
    "acc1_cash": -1.0,            // Costs cash
    "turnover_penalty": 1         // Small cost to discourage trading
  },
  "sell_acc1_VT": {
    "exposure_US_Equity": -0.60,
    "exposure_Intl_Equity": -0.40,
    "acc1_cash": 1.0,             // Generates cash
    "limit_acc1_VT": 1,           // Consumes inventory constraint
    "turnover_penalty": 1
  }
}

```

This effectively "flattens" the look-through problem. The solver sees `buy_acc1_VT` not as purchasing a ticker, but as simultaneously purchasing 0.6 units of US Equity and 0.4 units of International Equity. It will automatically choose `VT` over `VTI` (US only) if the portfolio needs both US and International exposure, optimizing the "bang for the buck" of the trade.3

### 4.3 Solver Performance and Web Workers

While `javascript-lp-solver` is efficient (solving 500+ variables in ~5ms 10), constructing the model for a user with 10 accounts and 50 assets can create thousands of variables and constraints. To ensure the "offline web app" remains responsive, the solving routine must be offloaded to a **Web Worker**. This prevents the UI from freezing during the optimization calculation.

Benchmarks suggest that for a "Monster LP" (552 variables, 600 constraints), the solver takes ~5ms. Even significantly scaling this up for a complex household portfolio (e.g., 2000 variables) keeps the execution time well under 1 second, making it perfectly viable for real-time client-side interaction.10

## 5. Algorithmic Approach II: Heuristic Methods

While Linear Programming offers mathematical robustness, Heuristic methods offer transparency ("explainability") and the ability to handle non-linear logic (like step-function transaction costs or complex tax brackets) that LP struggles with. The user query explicitly asks for "non-trivial adjustments (e.g., heuristics or iterative methods)," suggesting a need for approaches that might mimic human intuition or handle edge cases where LP is overkill or too rigid.

### 5.1 The Greedy Marginal Contribution Algorithm

This heuristic mimics a "steepest descent" approach. It iteratively executes the single best trade available until convergence.

**Mechanism:**

1.  **Calculate Drift:** Compute the dollar drift for every asset class $k$ ($D_k = \text{Actual}_k - \text{Target}_k$).
    
2.  **Identify Critical Class:** Find the class with the largest absolute drift ($k_{max}$).
    
3.  **Identify Best Instrument:** Search all accounts for the asset $i$ that:
    
    -   Has the highest exposure to class $k_{max}$.
        
    -   Is valid to trade (respects "Don't Sell"/"Don't Buy").
        
    -   Resides in an account with capacity (cash to buy, or inventory to sell).
        
4.  **Execute Micro-Trade:** "Buy" or "Sell" a small slice (e.g., 5% of the drift amount) of asset $i$.
    
5.  **Recalculate:** Update cash balances and exposures.
    
6.  **Repeat:** Loop until drifts are within a tolerance threshold or constraints prevent further moves.
    

Handling No-Transfer with "Swaps":

A purely greedy "Buy" might fail if an account has no cash. The heuristic must be modified to look for Swaps. Instead of evaluating atomic Buys/Sells, the algorithm evaluates pairs: "Sell Asset A / Buy Asset B" within Account $n$.

-   Score of Swap = (Reduction in Drift from Selling A) + (Reduction in Drift from Buying B).
    
-   The algorithm prioritizes swaps that solve two problems at once (e.g., selling an Overweight class to buy an Underweight class).
    

This heuristic is computationally inexpensive ($O(N \cdot M)$ per iteration) and easy to explain ("We sold A to buy B because A was overweight"). However, it risks getting stuck in local optima if no single swap improves the score, even if a 3-way trade would.12

### 5.2 Water-Filling with Priority Queues

Another non-trivial heuristic adapts the **Water-Filling Algorithm**, commonly used in communication channels.14

1.  **Buckets:** Treat Underweight Asset Classes as "buckets" that need filling.
    
2.  **Sources:** Treat Overweight Asset Classes as water sources.
    
3.  **Flow:** The algorithm attempts to flow capital from Sources to Buckets.
    
4.  **Constraint:** The flow is blocked by "Account Walls." Water can only flow from Source A to Bucket B if both exist in the same account (or if cash is available).
    

**Implementation:**

-   Create a list of **Sourcing Opportunities**: Assets that are overweight and sellable.
    
-   Create a list of **Sinking Opportunities**: Assets that are underweight and buyable.
    
-   **Match:** Iterate through Accounts. For Account $n$, match the best Source (highest overweight) with the best Sink (highest underweight).
    
-   **Iterate:** Continue matching until no valid Source-Sink pairs remain in any account.
    

This approach naturally handles the "no-transfer" constraint by processing matches strictly within account contexts. It effectively performs a **Coordinate Descent** optimization, optimizing one account at a time to improve the global objective.15

### 5.3 Comparison: LP vs. Heuristics

**Feature**

**Linear Programming (LP)**

**Heuristic (Greedy/Water-Filling)**

**Optimality**

**High.** Guarantees the mathematically best allocation given constraints.

**Medium.** May find local optima; "good enough" but not perfect.

**Look-Through**

**Native.** Handles complex composite assets effortlessly via matrix constraints.

**Complex.** Requires constant recalculation of aggregate exposure after every micro-step.

**Complexity**

**Setup-Heavy.** Requires building complex matrices/JSON models.

**Logic-Heavy.** Requires writing intricate loops and conditional logic.

**Low.** "The solver found this solution." Hard to trace specific "why".

For the "offline web app" requirement, **LP is generally recommended** for its code stability—once the model builder is written, the solver handles the complexity. Heuristics require maintaining complex state logic which is prone to bugs in edge cases (e.g., circular dependencies in swaps).

## 6. Advanced Constraints and "Non-Trivial" Adjustments

To truly "effectively reduce the gap," the algorithm must go beyond basic balancing and incorporate practical financial logic.

### 6.1 Tax-Aware "Shadow Costs" (Asset Location Optimization)

Although the prompt implies a focus on rebalancing, in a multi-account setup, **Asset Location** is the hidden driver of value. We want to place tax-inefficient assets (e.g., Bonds yielding ordinary income) in IRAs, and high-growth assets in Tax-Free (Roth) or Taxable accounts (for step-up basis).17

Implementation:

We can implement this via Shadow Costs in the LP or Weights in the Heuristic.

-   Assign a "Location Score" to every (Asset, Account) pair.
    
    -   Bond in IRA: Score 1.0 (Ideal).
        
    -   Bond in Taxable: Score 0.5 (Sub-optimal).
        
-   **LP Modification:** Add a penalty term to the objective function: $+ \lambda \sum (Buy_{n,i} \times \text{TaxPenalty}_{n,i})$.
    
    -   If the solver needs to buy Bonds, it effectively "costs" more to buy them in the Taxable account. The solver will naturally exhaust the IRA capacity first before resorting to the Taxable account.
        
    -   This is a "non-trivial adjustment" that optimizes _after-tax_ wealth without requiring a full tax engine.
        

### 6.2 Threshold Rebalancing (Drift Bands)

Continual rebalancing incurs transaction costs and tax events. A "non-trivial" algorithm should only rebalance if the drift exceeds a threshold (e.g., 5%).

-   LP Implementation: Instead of Exposure == Target, use Range Constraints:
    
    $$0.95 \cdot Target \le Exposure \le 1.05 \cdot Target$$
    
    The solver will do nothing if the current state is within the bounds (Objective cost = 0). If outside, it will trade just enough to reach the boundary, minimizing turnover.1
    

### 6.3 Handling "Wash Sales" Logic

A "Don't Buy" constraint is often used to prevent Wash Sales (buying a security 30 days before/after selling it at a loss).

-   **Automated Substitution:** If Asset A is marked "Don't Buy" (due to wash sale), the algorithm should automatically identify a **Substitute Asset B** (highly correlated but distinct ticker) within the same look-through class.
    
-   **Heuristic:** If the Greedy algorithm wants to buy Asset A but finds it locked, it scans for Asset B in the same account with similar exposure vector $E$ and routes the capital there.

### 7.2 Integration with `javascript-lp-solver`

The solver expects a flattened object model. The conversion function `buildModel()` must iterate through `accounts` and `assetMaster` to generate unique variable keys (e.g., `buy_Taxable_VT`).

Numerical Stability:

JavaScript uses floating-point math (number). For financial calculations, it is crucial to either:

1.  Use a Decimal library (e.g., `decimal.js`).
    
2.  Convert all values to **Integers (cents)** before passing to the solver to avoid precision errors (e.g., `0.1 + 0.2!= 0.3`). The `javascript-lp-solver` handles integers well.
    

### 7.3 Iterative Rounding (Dealing with Integer Shares)

LP solvers output continuous variables (e.g., Buy $100.50 of Stock). You cannot buy partial shares on many platforms.

Heuristic Adjustment:

1.  Run LP to get optimal dollar amounts.
    
2.  Convert to shares: `Math.floor(DollarAmount / SharePrice)`.
    
3.  Calculate "Leftover Cash" resulting from rounding down.
    
4.  **Second Pass (Greedy):** Use the leftover cash to buy 1 additional share of the asset with the highest remaining drift (highest marginal utility). This two-step process (LP + Greedy Clean-up) is a standard industry practice for "Integer Linear Programming" approximation.11

## 8. Conclusion

Solving the multi-account portfolio rebalancing problem with look-through capability and strict account constraints requires a hybrid algorithmic approach. While purely heuristic methods (greedy swapping) are intuitive and easy to implement, they struggle with the complex interdependencies of composite assets (ETFs). **Linear Programming (LP)** utilizing the **L1-norm minimization** of allocation drift stands out as the superior architecture for a robust, offline web application.

By modeling the problem as an LP with **elastic constraints** (slack variables) for drift and **hard constraints** for account balances and lock-ups, we can guarantee that the system finds the mathematically optimal "best effort" solution. It respects the "silo" nature of the accounts while piercing the veil of tickers to optimize the true underlying risk exposures.

The integration of `javascript-lp-solver` allows this heavy lifting to occur entirely client-side, respecting user privacy and offline requirements. When augmented with **Shadow Costs** for tax location and **Iterative Rounding** for share lots, the result is a professional-grade rebalancing engine that offers significant value over simple spreadsheet calculations.

**Recommendation:** Developers should prioritize the LP formulation for the core engine, using the `javascript-lp-solver` library wrapped in a Web Worker, and overlay a "Swap-based" heuristic only for generating human-readable explanations of the suggested trades.
